1.2 Mass Spectra of Elements

Mass spectra is used to identify the isotopes of an element as well as their relative abundances in nature. Isotopes are the same element and usually have somewhat similar properties, but differ slightly in the number of neutrons, and in turn, atomic mass.

Example: Given the mass spectra of Cl above, there are two isotopes: Cl-35 and Cl-37. Because about 75% of Cl atoms are Cl-35 and 25% are Cl-37, the average atomic mass is 35.45 g. This is the atomic mass that is shown on the periodic table

Example: With a mass spectra of an unknown element, the element can be inferred from the relative abundance of each mass peak. Based on the mass spectra above, the average mass is likely closest to 91 g, which would be closest to Zr on the periodic table (91.22 g).
